Lesson 3: Nouns and Articles

Understanding German Nouns

Welcome to Lesson 3! Here, we will dive into one of the most important topics in German grammar: nouns. We will learn about their genders and how to form plurals.

[ဘာသာပြန်]

Noun Genders and Definite Articles ("The")

As we learned in the grammar reference, every German noun has a gender: masculine, feminine, or neuter. You can tell the gender by its definite article (the word for "the").

  • Masculine: der
  • Feminine: die
  • Neuter: das

Important: The grammatical gender is often not logical. For example, the word for girl, das Mädchen, is neuter. You must memorize the article with the noun!

Examples:

Masculine (der) Feminine (die) Neuter (das)
der Stuhl (the chair)die Lampe (the lamp)das Buch (the book)
der Tisch (the table)die Tasche (the bag)das Auto (the car)
der Apfel (the apple)die Schule (the school)das Haus (the house)

Indefinite Articles ("A" or "An")

The indefinite article ("a" or "an") also changes based on the noun's gender.

  • For masculine nouns (der), use ein. (e.g., ein Stuhl - a chair)
  • For feminine nouns (die), use eine. (e.g., eine Lampe - a lamp)
  • For neuter nouns (das), use ein. (e.g., ein Buch - a book)

Notice that masculine and neuter nouns use the same indefinite article, "ein".

Plural Nouns

Forming plurals in German is more complex than in English. There are several different endings, and you need to learn the plural form for each noun. The good news is that in the plural, the definite article ("the") is always die for all genders.

Common Plural Endings:

Plural Ending Singular Plural
-e der Tisch (the table) die Tische (the tables)
-er das Buch (the book) die Bücher (the books)
-n / -en die Lampe (the lamp) die Lampen (the lamps)
-s (often for foreign words) das Auto (the car) die Autos (the cars)
No ending (sometimes with an umlaut change) der Apfel (the apple) die Äpfel (the apples)
